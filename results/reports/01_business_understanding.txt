Stage: Business Understanding

Research problem statement:
Digital waqf platforms improve accessibility, transparency, and operational efficiency, but they also increase exposure to fraudulent transaction behavior that can erode stakeholder trust.

Research objectives:
1. Build a reproducible machine learning workflow for fraud detection in a digital waqf proxy setting.
2. Assess how well fraud detection methods identify suspicious transactions under severe class imbalance.
3. Translate model findings into governance, trust, and transparency implications for digital waqf platforms.

Fraud risk assumptions in digital waqf platforms:
1. Suspicious behavior may involve unusual donation sizes, timing anomalies, repeated micro-transactions, or identity/device inconsistencies.
2. Fraud is rare relative to legitimate traffic, so class imbalance is expected to be extreme.
3. False negatives threaten trust and governance, while false positives may disrupt legitimate donors or administrators.

Proxy dataset rationale:
The available dataset is a public credit card fraud dataset rather than a waqf-native transaction dataset. It is still useful as a proxy because it captures highly imbalanced binary fraud detection behavior and allows evaluation of supervised and anomaly-based methods under realistic rarity conditions.

Success criteria:
1. Produce saved, machine-readable research artifacts for every stage.
2. Compare at least one supervised path and one anomaly-detection path.
3. Keep the workflow reproducible and ready for extension to richer transaction data.

Limitations and threats to validity:
1. The proxy dataset does not represent actual waqf transaction semantics.
2. Features are anonymized, so domain interpretation is limited.
3. Conclusions about digital waqf should be framed as method-transfer insights, not direct operational evidence.
